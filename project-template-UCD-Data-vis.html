Twitter Analytical Tool

Time-Series Word Cloud

Data Source:
Donald Trumps Tweets
Anonymous Twitter Bot

Domain Tasks
- What does this user care about ?
- How can I decipher if this user is real or not?
- Has this user's interests and views changed over time ?

Motivation
In recent years Twitter has become one of the most prominent areas of public discourse online. Following the 2016 US presidential election, Twitter, along with other platforms such as Facebook, came under pressure with how they managed the spread of fake news during this election, and what efforts were being made to avoid future political interference from both domestic and foreign entities. While internally, twitter has been utilising both machine learning and automation technologies to stop the spread of disinformation, it has provided a minimal amount of analytical tools for it’s users to decipher such information and accounts. A recent study by the Knight Foundation [1] highlighted the different ways fake news spreads across Twitter. One of the main indicators of bot activity was around repetition of clustered tweets and hashtags on a regular basis, and while this activity can be discovered easily through an extracted dataset, user’s may struggle to identify this behaviour through Twitter's current timeline functionality.

Research, Design and Development
Traditional text-based visual representations are commonly used to highlight importance, where the frequency of a word is associated with either it’s font-size or font weight [2,3]. Additional attributes such as colour and algorithmic layout adjustments [2] have been used to further enhance these visualizations, as shown in Figure 1, 2, through the reducedation of whitespace, appropriate spacing, and the usage of a contrasting colour palette [6] (Figure 4). Criticism of word clouds have been mostly grounded in the efficiency and accuracy of the processing and extraction of data. Applicational scenarios such as user generated reviews have demonstrated how information can be missed or hidden by the lack of adjectives accompanying nouns within a cloud, and how inconsistent writing styles can increase the amount of effort and time to read information, potentially interfering with user’s overall perception of a given dataset [5]. While not questioning the validity of the criticism, word clouds have seen success in other non-descriptive heavy contexts where information isn’t generated by various users, and is often used within a specific topical area.

This visualisation was formed from datasets that were made up of three main features: hashtag characters, date/time, and frequency count. The first two visualisations (Figure 1,2) present how a timeline of hashtag usage could be displayed within Twitter's analytics dashboards. With the first dashboard (Figure 1) highlighting a timeline of Donald Trump's hashtag usage since 2016, accompanied with a secondary visualisation (Figure 2) based upon an anonymous twitter bots hashtag usage across the same time period. Initially, the design of the cloud attempted to include all of the hashtags used across multiple years. However, it quickly became apparent that this would lead to information overload and result in a large amount of the dataset being hidden. To ensure that the user would be able to distinguish the different levels of information, I referred to Munzner and Maguire’s [6] discriminability guidelines which led to the refactoring of the design. As a result, the focus of the design now shifted towards a more traditional horizontal time-series consisting of word cloud containers based upon year, most importantly this addressed both domain-task two and three, while also increasing the range of data users could access without being overloaded. While the time-series layout improved the user’s ability to distinguish weighting across time, other concerns around visibility arose. Taking influence from Ben Shneiderman’s Overview First, Zoom and Filter, Details on Demand [7] a secondary toggle view was introduced as shown in Figure 3, with each visualization - embedded within a vertical scrollable view - taking advantage of the full width of the screen. This would ensure users are initially provided with all of the items within the dataset simultaneously, with the secondary ability to draw a more detailed view of the given visualization.

The D3.js framework was used to create the visualisation, during the development of the word cloud two main objectives were achieved. First, efforts around algorithmic adjustments to the layout and positioning of each word provided the ability to control both the density and space-filling of each visualisation, which directly influenced what kinds of visual encoding where used [6] and avoided common issues around overlapping. Secondarily, both the design and development took a mobile-first approach considering the medium the visualisation would be used within. When considering what kinds of design choices where needed to fulfill domain-task one, interactivity was frequently used across some of the early sketches. Expecting user's to become interested in more abstract data, which they might know little about, additional functionality around exploration and analysis was added. As shown in Figure.6, following the recommended interactivity mechanisms of selecting and linking [9], each word inside the cloud would be a selectable element - applying an alterative contrasted hex value to the rest of the palette [6] - this would then be linked to a side menu that could expand and collapse each tweet that the selected hashtag had be used within. Providing users the ability to both, see the increase or decrease of frequency of usage throughout a timeline and the ability to gain a deeper contextual level of affordance with the drilling down into individual tweets.

UK COVID-19 Report

Sparkline Map & Bar Chart

Data Source:
Office For National Statistics(https://www.ons.gov.uk/)
NHS England (https://www.england.nhs.uk/statistics/statistical-work-areas/covid-19-dailydeaths/)

Medium:
This visualisation has been designed as a multimedia report that could be used both in both public or digital spaces.

Research, Design and Development:
This report consists of two main visualutions. First, a geographical representation of the UK, with England regions contrasted to the foreground. Accompanied with line graphs, representing death count (per day), overlaid across the relative regions. The second visualization focused on regional totals through a combination of contrasting labels presenting case and death counts with an additional bar chart focused solely on death counts, providing a cumulative total to support the first visualization. It's suggested that maps often feel personal to users, and tend to be an intuitive medium across all generations [10]. Initial designs focused on extracting datasets based on English local authorities to ensure that the visualution addressed domain task one. However, after multiple iterations of the sketches and prototypes, as shown in Figure 8 and 9, the amount of data being shown resulted in the inability to distinguish each data node. To avoid interchannel interference, I evolved the design to reduce the scale of the dataset to regional statistics, as I felt this still addressed the domain tasks focus of providing local data. To improve the discriminability and visual accuracy of each channel, I altered the hue of both the background and the line channel to ensure the contrast provided a magnified view of the data being presented [6]. Additional channels were added to support the overall structure of the graph's x and y axis's, improving separability and overlap of each graph [6]. Regional labels were also added, as shown in Figure.7, to assist users who may be unfamiliar with the geographical area, while also improving the speed- accuracy trade offs that familiar users may face. Considerations were also made around the different ways users could interact with web-based reports as highlighted in Figure.12. Allowing users to toggle between different geographical and quantitative parameters.

World COVID-19 Breakdown

Mirrored Histogram

Data Source:
Johns Hopkins University (JHU), https://github.com/CSSEGISandData/COVID-19/

Medium:
This visualisation would mostly likely be a stand alone website with the aim of being mobile accessible. Additional functionality around API's could enable this visualization to be shared through iframes to news outlets and educational organizations.

Domain Tasks:
- Which country has been most effected by COVID-19? - What stage of infection is a country currently at ? - How success/unsuccess have countries been with suppressing new cases ?

Research, Design and Development:
When visualising the COVID-19 data the aim was to be able to create a distinguishable combined view of the multiple death and case variables. Examples shown in figure 16 show the early ideations around managing multiple conditions within visualisations. The final design as shown in figure.15 settled on a mirrored histogram approach, where the vertical axis shows the gradient magnitude of the old and new confirmed cases, recovery's, and deaths. This data could of been a presented as line graph as shown in figure 16. While the overall outline of the projected data would of looked similar, the layout of the mirrored histogram provided a powerful silhouette of the shape of the data. Emphasising multiple levels of information such as; the stage a country was in relation to infections and deaths, how much had cases and deaths increased or decreased over the given time period, and what countries had been successful in managing the outbreaks of infections. Histograms are a common technique to present the distribution of continuous data through non-overlapping slabs, which are usually consistent in size and orientation [9]. The visualisation referred to Munzners Separate, Order, and Align: Categorical Regions [6] when refining the later designs. As shown in figure 15, the design reflects the usage of spatial regions between each vertical slab, followed by additions of color hue and a singular key. Embellishments around country flags (figure 15) were used to enhance user retention and their ability to recall information in the long-term [12]. This also is suggested to make the data feel more familiar and personal to the individual user [10]. The data was stored as an CSV file which was then imported to D3.js where it was manipulated and structured. The visualisation was then outputted into a web page, formatted through HTML and CSS.



Nuclear Explosions Since 1945

Bubble Map

Medium:
This visualisation would mostly likely be a stand alone video or gif that could be shared online, or embedded within a website with additional interactive functionality.

Domain Tasks:
- Inform the user about the scale of nuclear explosions used across the world. - Emphasise the difference between the usage of nuclear bombs in the past and now. - When was nuclear weapons tested the most ?

Research, Design and Development:
This visualization takes form as a geographical representation of the world, showing an animated timeline of every nuclear bomb used around the world. The dataset consisted of four main features; date and time, location, country name, and x/y coordinates. One of the most important aspects of this visualization was ensuring that it made the data feel personal to the individual, as Yau suggest that maps feel personal to people as a result of their familiarity [10]. As the nature of the data was around specific geographic coordinates, it was felt that a bubble design would serve this visualization best as each of the red circles, representing a nuclear explosion, are displayed over a designated geographical region. As bubble charts are widely acknowledged to be an effective way of comparing proportions across geographical regions, compared to other alternatives such as choropleth maps, which can often mislead users perceptions around the proportion of a given value as larger regions appear more emphasised then smaller ones [10]. Within a web-based medium this visualization would look something similar to figure 18, once again referring to Yau suggestions on how using multiple maps together can help show patters across time series data.
